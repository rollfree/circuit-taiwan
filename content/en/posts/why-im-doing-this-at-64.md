People look at the 1,200 km route map of the Circuit Taiwan expedition. Then they look at me. Then they look back at the map. 

Eventually, they ask the same question: *"Why now? Why at 64?"*

It is a fair question. Society has a very specific, unspoken timeline for us. By 64, the expectation is that you have finished your major physical endeavors. You are supposed to be slowing down, securing your comfort, and letting the younger generation take the risks. 

But here is the truth: I am not doing this *despite* being 64. I am doing this *because* I am 64.

### The Vantage Point of Experience

When I first drove around Taiwan 25 years ago, I was young, fast, and entirely focused on the destination. I saw the island through a windshield. 

Today, my mobility has changed. I navigate the world from a wheelchair. But my ambition has not shrunk; it has simply refined. 

At 64, you no longer have the energy to waste on things that don't matter. You don't do a 35-day expedition for ego, or for a midlife crisis, or to prove something to people who don't understand you. You do it because you have spent four decades living in this incredible country, and you finally have the clarity to see what needs to be done.

### A Wheelchair is a Lens, Not a Limit

When you move at 15 km/h instead of 100 km/h, the world changes. 

A car allows you to ignore the environment. A wheelchair forces you to interact with it. You notice the broken sidewalk, the steep ramp, the lack of accessible restrooms, and the kindness of strangers who stop to help. 

I am doing this to document that reality. I want to map the true accessibility of Taiwan's coast, not from the perspective of an able-bodied tourist, but from the perspective of someone who actually needs the infrastructure to work. 

### The Synthesis of a Lifetime

Circuit Taiwan is not just a physical ride. It is the synthesis of everything I have learned over 40 years in Taiwan. 

It combines my background in engineering to build an offline, AI-driven safety system for the Su-Hua Highway. It combines my respect for the indigenous communities of the East Coast, whose stories I want to document in three languages. It combines my belief that adaptive mobility should be backed by world-class technology.

None of this could have been done when I was 30. I didn't have the patience, the technical maturity, or the deep understanding of the culture required to pull it off. 

### To the Adaptive Community

If you are reading this and you have recently transitioned to a wheelchair, or you are facing a physical limitation that feels like an ending: this expedition is for you. 

I want to prove that a change in mobility is not a reduction in life. It is a change in perspective. The road is still there. The horizon is still waiting. You just have to engineer a new way to reach it.

### The Road Ahead

I am 64. My body is not what it used to be. I will get tired. The subtropical heat will be brutal. The Su-Hua cliffs will be intimidating. 

But I have the right team, the right technology, and the right mindset. I am not in a rush. I am not trying to set a speed record. I am simply trying to roll forward, one kilometer at a time, and show the world what is possible when you refuse to let age dictate your boundaries.

See you on the road.
