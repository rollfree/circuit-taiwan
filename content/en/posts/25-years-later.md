Twenty-five years ago, I drove around Taiwan in a car. The roads were narrower, the Su-Hua Highway still carved raw into cliff faces, and I saw the island at 80 km/h through a windshield.

Now, I am returning — at 10 km/h, from wheelchair height.

This is not a nostalgia trip. It is an engineering project, a medical field study, and a bilingual story about how Taiwan has transformed.

## Why This Matters

- The Su-Hua Highway Improvement Project (蘇花改) was completed in 2021 — the route is newly viable for slow, non-motorised travel.
- Miniaturised lightning detection (AS3935) + real-time weather APIs now enable a safety system that was impossible even ten years ago.
- No systematic wheelchair-level accessibility data exists for Taiwan's coastal highways. We will create it — and release it under Creative Commons.

## What You'll Find Here

- 🛠️ Engineering logs: lightning detection, LTE dead zones, battery performance in monsoon conditions
- 🩺 Medical telemetry: thermoregulation, fatigue, cardiovascular response in sustained tropical exertion
- 🗺️ Accessibility maps: surface quality, shelter locations, gradient data — all downloadable
- 📖 Bilingual narrative: Taiwan seen at wheelchair height, 25 years later
