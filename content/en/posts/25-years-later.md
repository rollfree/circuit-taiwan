Twenty-five years ago, I drove around Taiwan in a car. The roads were narrower, the Su-Hua Highway still carved raw into cliff faces, and I saw the island at 80 km/h through a windshield.

Now, I am returning — at 10 km/h, from wheelchair height.

This is not a nostalgia trip. It is an engineering project, a medical field study, and a bilingual story about how Taiwan has transformed.

## 


## Why This Matters and Why This Has Not Been Done Before

Three independent developments have converged to make Circuit Taiwan not just possible, but necessary — for the first time:

### 1. The Infrastructure Window: Su-Hua Is Finally Viable
The Su-Hua Highway Improvement Project (蘇花改) completed in 2021 did more than widen a road — it unlocked Taiwan's east coast for slow, non-motorised, and adaptive travel for the first time in history. What was once fragmented and impassable is now a continuous, legally recognised slow-vehicle corridor. The route is open. The timing is now.

### 2. The Technology Threshold: Safety Systems That Actually Work
Miniaturised lightning detection (AS3935), edge-computing weather stations, and real-time API integration have crossed a critical threshold. We can now deploy a field-proven, offline-capable early-warning system that simply did not exist ten years ago. This isn't theoretical engineering — it's operational reality, ready for tropical deployment.

### 3. The Unmet Need: Zero Wheelchair-Level Data Exists
Despite this new viability, **no systematic, wheelchair-accessibility database exists for Taiwan's 1,200 km coastal highway network.** Adaptive travellers, future expeditions, and infrastructure planners are navigating blind.

### The Deliverable: First-Mover Advantage in Open-Source Mobility Data
Circuit Taiwan will map every segment — surface gradients, shelter locations, charging points, risk zones, and accessibility ratings — and publish it as a **Creative Commons, wheelchair-verified dataset**.

This is not just an expedition. It is the **foundational infrastructure map** that will define adaptive mobility in Taiwan for the next decade. Sponsors and technical partners won't just be associated with a journey — they will be named in the dataset, the engineering reports, and the open-source safety architecture that enables future travel.

**First-mover credibility. Verifiable impact. Lasting legacy.**

> The window is open. The technology is ready. The gap is real. We are building the first verified, wheelchair-level map of Taiwan's coast — and the safety architecture that makes it possible.
