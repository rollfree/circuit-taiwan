Why Local Hotels Beat B&Bs for our Expedition

The surprising truth about accessibility, logistics, and value for multi-day adaptive travel.

Here’s something that surprised us during Circuit Taiwan planning: B&Bs and short-term rentals are often more expensive than established hotels—and they rarely offer the accessibility infrastructure we need.

This isn’t a criticism of B&Bs. They’re wonderful for many travelers. But for a 35-day adaptive mobility expedition with a wheelchair user, professional carer, driver, and writer, the math and logistics point decisively toward hotels.

The Cost Reality#

The surprise: When you need 2–3 rooms for a team, or an entire accessible unit, B&Bs lose their “budget-friendly” advantage fast.

Why Hotels Win for Adaptive Travel#

✅ Accessibility Infrastructure#

Hotels built after Taiwan’s accessibility regulations have:

  • Elevator access to all floors
  • Roll-in showers with grab bars (not just a “step-free entrance”)
  • Wide doorways (80cm+ standard in accessible rooms)
  • Emergency evacuation protocols for guests with disabilities
  • Staff trained in accessibility assistance

B&Bs? Often retrofitted old buildings with narrow stairs, no elevator, and “we’ll help you carry the wheelchair” as the accessibility plan.

✅ CSR and Partnership Value#

Hotels understand corporate social responsibility and long-term partnerships:

  • They can offer negotiated rates for named partnership status
  • They receive accessibility certification from our wheelchair-user audit
  • They gain trilingual content appearing in daily logs, social media, and documentary
  • They build CSR case studies for their own marketing

A B&B owner might see us as “nice guests.” A hotel sees us as brand development and community inclusion storytelling.

What We Ask of Hotel Partners#

Our requirements are clear and non-negotiable:

  1. Accessible ground-floor or elevator-access room for expedition leader (wheelchair user)
  2. Vehicle parking for assistance van with equipment
  3. Charging point for wheelchair batteries and electronics
  4. Complimentary accommodation for 4 team members (framed as in-kind partnership)

In return, hotels receive:

  • Professional accessibility audit (published as open data)
  • Daily trilingual content from their location
  • Named “Official Accommodation Partner” status
  • Documentary mention if visit produces significant content

Daily Operations: The Other 27 Nights#

Named partners cover key strategic stops. The remaining nights require a dynamic, flexible booking strategy. Here is how we handle daily operations:

  • Direct Negotiation: We bypass third-party platforms. We contact managers directly, explain the adaptive context, and often secure better rates by booking during shoulder season or waiving daily housekeeping.
  • Refundable-Only Policy: All bookings use free-cancellation rates, allowing us to pivot instantly for typhoon delays, route changes, or rider fatigue.
  • Baseline Standards Apply: Even without a partnership, every stay must meet our non-negotiable criteria: accessible room, vehicle parking, power access, and 24-hour reception or secure late-check-in.
  • Data for the Community: Every hotel we stay at contributes to our open-source accessibility database. We document door widths, ramp gradients, and charging availability, publishing the results freely for future adaptive travelers.## The Bottom Line

B&Bs are lovely for couples on weekend getaways. But for serious adaptive travel—multi-day expeditions with equipment, vehicles, and accessibility needs—established hotels deliver better value, better infrastructure, and better partnership potential.

We’re not looking for luxury. We’re looking for reliability, accessibility, and genuine partnership. And that’s what Taiwan’s mid-range hotel sector delivers.
